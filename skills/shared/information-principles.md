# Information Principles: Octave Content

How to structure, organize, and present information for maximum clarity and impact. These principles apply regardless of format (document, deck, one-pager, microsite) and are independent of both visual design and language quality.

Visual design determines how content LOOKS. Language rules determine how it READS. These principles determine how it's ORGANIZED and what it SAYS.

**Floor vs. craft.** Some principles here are structural floors: lead with the conclusion, no overlaps, claims backed by evidence. Violations are always problems. Others are craft aspirations: three-layer hierarchy, named narrative arcs, source attribution. Apply craft standards when the content naturally supports them. Don't force a three-layer pyramid onto a section with one clean insight, and don't add source notes to qualitative observations. If applying a principle makes the output feel over-structured or stiff, skip it.

## Structure

1. **Lead with the conclusion.** State the main finding, recommendation, or takeaway first. Supporting evidence follows. The reader should know the "so what" before the "here's why." Don't make the audience sit through the entire analysis to reach the point: give them the point, then prove it.

   Structure content in three layers:
   - **Main point** (headline/title): the takeaway the reader should walk away with
   - **Key arguments** (2-4 supporting reasons): why the main point is true
   - **Evidence** (data, examples, observations): proof for each key argument

   Each layer summarizes everything below it. If a piece of evidence doesn't support a key argument, or a key argument doesn't support the main point, it doesn't belong.

2. **No overlaps, no gaps.** Every section, argument, or data point should be distinct (no two sections saying the same thing) and collectively complete (no obvious missing piece the reader would expect). If a reader asks "but what about X?" and X is critical, there's a gap. If two sections make the same point with different words, there's an overlap. A personalized restatement of a generic point is still the same point, it counts as an overlap. Fold personalization into the section where the point first appears.

3. **Every headline is a claim.** Section headings, chart titles, and stat labels state the takeaway, not the topic. "Enterprise prospects frame pricing as a team-size problem" not "Pricing." The reader can agree or disagree with a claim; they can't agree or disagree with a label.

   **Exception: structural section labels.** In dense, single-page formats (one-pagers, leave-behinds), sections may use short structural labels ("The Challenge," "How It Works," "Results") for navigation. These are structural signposts, not content headings, and are exempt from the claims rule. The claim should live in the opening sentence or card headings within the section. Use label voice (uppercase, letterspaced) to visually distinguish section labels from content headings.

4. **Narrative arc, not information dump.** Content follows a logical progression that builds toward a conclusion. Common structures:
   - **Problem → Evidence → Recommendation** (analytical)
   - **Situation → Complication → Question → Answer** (persuasive, McKinsey SCQA; the Question step makes the hypothesis explicit before presenting evidence)
   - **Before → Change → After** (comparative)
   - **Pattern → Implication → Action** (intelligence)

   Pick the arc that fits the content. Never present findings as an unstructured list.

5. **Executive summary answers the big questions first.** If the document has an opening summary or overview section, it must answer: What did we find? Why does it matter? What should we do? Decision-makers may only read this section: it must stand alone.

## Content Quality

6. **Every claim is backed by evidence.** No unsupported assertions. Evidence can be quantitative (stats, percentages, counts) or qualitative (conversation patterns, customer quotes, market observations). If you can't point to the evidence, cut the claim. When citing specific data, include source attribution: a small source note adds credibility and lets the reader verify.

6b. **Show the receipts.** When a claim summarizes conversations or deals, the evidence must be specific and inspectable, inline:
   - **Count and window.** "Across 13 conversations from June 1–30," never a bare "across recent conversations" when the underlying data is countable.
   - **Named examples.** 2-3 real account names listed inline: "(with accounts like Acme, SaaSco, and Broker)". Real humans with titles where available.
   - **Verbatim language, when the claim is about what people said.** A claim about what "prospects say" without quoted words is an assertion, not evidence: back it with a full quote or the short 2-3 word phrases customers actually used. Quantitative claims (rates, sizes, frequencies) are evidenced by counts and baselines, don't force a quote onto them.
   - **Comparable frequency.** When reporting that a topic came up, report how often, and give the counts for sibling topics so the reader can compare frequency across topics, not just trust that one is "common."

   Template: *"Across 13 conversations (June 1–30, incl. Acme, SaaSco, Broker), zoning analysis came up unprompted in 9, more than any other capability ('this would save my analyst a week', VP Acquisitions, Acme)."* Vague summary prose that could have been written without reading the calls is the failure mode.

   **Precision must be honest.** Receipts at this level apply where the underlying data is specific and countable. Some valuable insights are synthesized patterns across many touchpoints that don't reduce to a clean count or a single quote: keep them if they're sharp, evidence them with representative examples and directional framing ("a recurring theme in Q2 enterprise conversations"), and let the hedged conviction (#12) signal their strength. Never manufacture precision (invented counts, forced quotes) to satisfy this rule; false precision is worse than honest generality.

6c. **Quote fidelity beats quote quantity.** A wrong or out-of-context quote does more damage than no quote: it's the one thing a reader can check against the source. Quote only what's verbatim and whose meaning survives extraction; when unsure, downgrade to a short quoted phrase or unquoted paraphrase rather than forcing it. Attribute only as precisely as you're certain (person + title if confirmed, otherwise the account). A receipt without a full quote is still a receipt: the count and named accounts carry it.

7. **Data tells a story, not just a number.** Raw numbers require framing: what changed and against what baseline. A chart without a headline that states the takeaway is decoration.

7b. **Interpretation must earn its place.** After the claim and its evidence, stop. Each additional paragraph must add NEW information (a mechanism, a nuance, a comparison), not restate the claim in different words or append generic advice. Readers of intelligence content want the data; interpretation that could have been written without the data is filler and erodes trust in the parts that are real. Recommendations are welcome only when they are specific and non-obvious (see #9); when in doubt, deliver the information and let the reader conclude.

   This applies doubly to seller-facing documents. The recurring AI pattern is (1) a piece of account information followed by (2) a suggestion for how to act on it, and the suggestion can usually be deleted. Reps know their job; instructions they'd give themselves ("worth uncovering in discovery," "position against this by...") are drag that stops them reading. Deliver the intelligence lean. Teams that want heavier handholding can add it in their own skill customization.

   **Evidence section headers frame, they don't compete.** When a section presents multiple proof points or metrics, the section header should frame or summarize the evidence below, not make its own specific data claim that competes with the stats. "Acquisitions teams cut research costs 78% and doubled deal velocity" as a header, followed by three metrics that prove similar points, creates noise. The header should orient ("Results") or summarize ("Teams have cut costs and increased velocity"), and let the evidence below do the proving.

8. **Cut the obvious.** If the audience would say "yeah, we know," the content isn't earning its space. Table-stakes information (basic product facts, well-known industry trends) should only appear when it's reframed or challenged by new evidence.

8b. **No generic padding.** When real data is thin for a particular angle, don't pad the output with generic AI content to fill space. A shorter, grounded document is more valuable than a longer, generic one. Listing 10 hypothetical objections when only 2 have surfaced in conversations, or writing 3 paragraphs of industry analysis when no field data exists, signals AI generation. Lead with the real data, note the sample is small, move on.

9. **Recommendations are specific and actionable.** Every recommendation points to something the team can change: messaging, positioning, enablement, materials, process. "Build a pricing calculator that frames cost per protected identity" is actionable. "Have better conversations" is not.

9b. **Outcome-driven, not process-driven.** Goals and recommendations state exit criteria: what should be true when this is done ("leave with a confirmed next step and a technical champion identified"), never a minute-by-minute script ("0-5 rapport, 5-20 discovery, 20-25 next steps"). A phase-by-phase clock is a process artifact, not a recommendation; readers need to know what to achieve, not when to check their watch.

10. **Order by impact.** Within any section, the most important point comes first. Importance is measured by surprise value, decision impact, or actionability, not chronology or convention.

11. **Data needs a reference point.** A number without context is trivia. "40% of prospects raise pricing" means nothing alone. Compare to a baseline: vs. last quarter, vs. another segment, vs. what you'd expect. The comparison IS the insight; the number is just the vehicle. When no direct comparison exists, frame the magnitude: "nearly half," "a minority," "the dominant pattern."

12. **Calibrate conviction to evidence.** Strong evidence gets definitive language ("prospects consistently report," "the pattern is clear"). Weak or emerging signals get hedged language ("early signs suggest," "a small number of conversations indicate"). Never overstate thin data, and never hedge strong patterns into mush. The reader should be able to gauge signal strength from the phrasing. When no data exists for an angle, say so rather than manufacturing confidence. An honest gap is more useful than an invented narrative.

12b. **Tag confirmed vs. hypothesized.** Calibrated conviction (#12) is about tone; this is about the label. Where a document mixes what's known with what's inferred, tag the difference explicitly: **Confirmed** = sourced from CRM data, call transcripts/findings, verified enrichment, or direct user input. **Hypothesized** = inferred from enrichment, persona patterns, or industry norms. Verify before you assert: a named person or title must trace to a real tool result before it's stated as fact, not carried over from a guess.

12c. **Entity deep-links to source (internal docs only).** In an internal, seller-facing document (meeting prep, brief, battlecard, deal coaching), a cited library entity (proof point, reference, persona, competitor, objection, use case, segment, Motion ICP cell) can link straight to its record via `https://app.octavehq.com/entity/{oId}`, which resolves in the reader's own workspace. Never put these links in a customer-facing asset (deck, one-pager, microsite, proposal): they're broken for the customer and leak internal tooling. If it's unclear whether a document is internal or customer-facing, leave the links out.

## Framing

13. **Frame as intelligence, not as output.** The reader should feel like they're receiving a brief from a sharp analyst, not a report from a software tool. No tool-output language ("findings analyzed," "data surfaced"), no system jargon, no internal process references.

14. **Match framing to audience.** The same information requires different framing for different readers:
    - **Executive** → conclusions, implications, decisions required. Compress evidence. Lead with the action.
    - **Practitioner** → evidence, patterns, specific guidance. Show the work. Be prescriptive.
    - **Technical** → methodology, precision, data integrity. Be exact. Show confidence intervals.

    Know who's reading and frame accordingly.

14b. **Keep their pain separate from your deal risk.** The customer's pain is their problem: their world, their words, framed from their point of view. Internal deal health (single-threaded, low activity, stalled stage) is your problem, not theirs; it belongs in deal context or a deal snapshot section, never folded into "their pain." Mixing the two muddies both: it makes the customer's problem sound like an internal complaint, and buries a real deal-risk signal inside content meant to build empathy for the buyer.

15. **One idea per unit.** Each card, slide, section, or content block conveys one concept. If a reader needs to hold two unrelated ideas simultaneously to understand a block, split it.

## Review Checklist

Information architecture audit. Run every check against the generated content. These checks apply to the CONTENT and ORGANIZATION, not the visual design or language quality.

### Structure

- [ ] **Conclusion first.** The main finding or recommendation appears early, not buried after pages of evidence.
- [ ] **Three-layer hierarchy (where depth supports it).** Content follows main point → key arguments → evidence. Each layer summarizes what's below it. No orphaned evidence that doesn't connect to a key argument. A section with one clean insight doesn't need to be forced into three layers.
- [ ] **No overlaps.** No two sections or content blocks make the same point. Each earns its space independently. Personalized restatements of a generic point count as overlaps.
- [ ] **No gaps.** The content addresses the obvious questions a reader would have. No critical missing pieces.
- [ ] **Headlines are claims.** Every heading, chart title, and stat label states a takeaway. Test: can the reader agree or disagree? Exception: structural section labels in one-pagers (label voice, 2-3 words) are navigation signposts, not claims.
- [ ] **Evidence headers frame, not compete.** Section headers for evidence/metrics sections summarize or orient rather than making specific data claims that compete with the stats below.
- [ ] **Narrative arc present.** Content follows a logical progression, not a random list. The reader feels guided, not dumped on. The arc can be implicit (clear ordering that builds); it doesn't need to map to a named framework.

### Content Quality

- [ ] **Claims backed by evidence.** No unsupported assertions. Every major point has data, a pattern, or a specific observation behind it. Specific data points have source attribution where applicable.
- [ ] **Receipts shown.** Claims about conversations/deals carry inline counts + date windows, 2-3 named accounts, and verbatim quotes or quoted phrases. No bare "across recent conversations" when the data is countable. Topic frequencies are comparable across topics.
- [ ] **Quotes are context-safe.** Every quote is verbatim from the source, meaning-preserving out of context, and correctly attributed. Shaky quotes are downgraded to quoted phrases or paraphrase, not forced.
- [ ] **No interpretation padding.** After claim + evidence, every additional paragraph adds new information. No restatement paragraphs, no generic advice blocks. Cut interpretation that could have been written without the data.
- [ ] **No generic padding.** Thin data sections are short. No filler paragraphs of generic industry analysis where real data is missing. Hypothetical objections or manufactured proof points flagged and cut.
- [ ] **Data framed.** Charts and stats have explanatory context: what changed, against what baseline.
- [ ] **No obvious content.** Nothing that would make the audience shrug. Every piece passes the "would this surprise the team?" test.
- [ ] **Recommendations are specific.** Each recommendation points to a concrete action, not generic advice.
- [ ] **Outcome-driven, not process-driven.** Goals and recommendations state exit criteria, not a phase-by-phase clock or minute-by-minute timeline.
- [ ] **Ordered by impact.** Within each section, the most important point appears first.
- [ ] **Data has context (where applicable).** Key numbers are compared to a baseline, benchmark, or expectation. Isolated numbers without reference points are flagged. Not every stat needs a comparison; use judgment.
- [ ] **Conviction matches evidence.** Strong patterns use definitive language. Weak signals are appropriately hedged. No overstatement of thin data, no hedging of clear patterns.
- [ ] **Confirmed vs. hypothesized tagged.** Where a document mixes known facts with inferred ones, the two are explicitly labeled. Named people/titles trace to a real tool result, not a guess.
- [ ] **Entity deep-links used correctly (internal docs only).** Library entity links (`app.octavehq.com/entity/{oId}`) appear only in internal, seller-facing documents, never in customer-facing assets.

### Framing

- [ ] **Intelligence, not output.** Content reads as market intelligence from an analyst, not a report from a tool.
- [ ] **Audience-appropriate.** Framing matches the stated audience (executive, practitioner, technical).
- [ ] **Pain vs. deal risk separated.** The customer's pain (their words, their world) is kept distinct from internal deal health signals (single-threaded, stalled, low activity), which live in deal context, not in "their pain."
- [ ] **One idea per unit.** No content block requires the reader to hold multiple unrelated concepts simultaneously.
