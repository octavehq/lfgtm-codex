# Format: One-Pager / Leave-Behind

Single-page or very short documents designed to be handed off, emailed, or printed. Everything the reader needs on one surface. Dense but scannable.

**Skills that produce this format:** one-pager, proposal (condensed mode)

These principles supplement the universal visual rules in `presentation-principles.md` (one directory up). The universal principles are the floor; these add format-specific requirements.

## Principles

1. **Print-first design.** The primary use case is as a PDF or printed handout. Screen viewing is secondary. Design for paper: light backgrounds, high contrast, no interactivity dependencies.

2. **Everything above the fold.** The core message, key stats, and call to action must be visible without scrolling. When printed, everything fits on one page (two at most for proposals). If it doesn't fit, cut, don't shrink.

3. **Density is managed, not avoided.** One-pagers pack more content per square inch than long-form documents. This is by design. But density must be MANAGED through hierarchy, grid layouts, and aggressive visual chunking, not just tolerated. Dense and scannable is the goal. Dense and overwhelming is failure.

4. **Grid-based layout.** Two-column or multi-column grids maximize information density while maintaining scannability. Content flows in a predictable grid, not freeform prose.

5. **Hierarchy does heavy lifting.** With more content in less space, the type hierarchy must be sharper than in long-form docs. The reader's eye path (headline → key stat → supporting detail → CTA) should be unmistakable on first glance.

5b. **Ledes sit a clear tier above card body text.** When a section opens with an intro line followed by cards or bullets, the intro must be visually superior: larger size and/or heavier weight. If the section intro and the card copy below it render at the same size, the hierarchy is broken and the section reads as an undifferentiated text block.

5c. **Sections need structural labels that feel human.** Each section uses a short label (2-5 words, label voice: uppercase, letterspaced) that tells the reader what kind of section they're looking at. Labels should be plain and conversational: the reader should feel oriented, not lectured. Prefer concrete, relatable labels ("The Problem Today," "How [Product] Helps," "What Teams Are Seeing," "Next Step") over academic or abstract ones ("The Challenge," "Our Approach," "Results"). Avoid cheeky/clever labels ("Sound Familiar?") and overly declarative ones ("What You're Up Against"). Claims live in the opening sentence or sub-headings within the section, not in the label.

6. **Co-branding prominent.** Logo placement, customer branding, and "powered by" are visible and professional. This document gets forwarded to people who don't know you. First impressions matter.

7. **No interactive elements.** No tabs, no modals, no hover states that reveal content. This must work as a static PDF. Every piece of information is visible without clicking anything.

8. **CTA or next steps visible.** The reader should finish knowing exactly what to do next. Call to action, contact info, or next steps are prominent, not buried in fine print.

9. **White space is surgical.** In long-form docs, white space is generous. In one-pagers, white space is precise: used to separate sections and create breathing room within tight constraints. Every margin is intentional.

10. **Fragments and bullets, not paragraphs.** Prose paragraphs belong in long-form documents. In a one-pager, section intros are one sentence max (~20 words), and everything else is bullets, cards with short subheaders, or stats. Any run of body text longer than one sentence gets converted to bullets or cut by a third. CTAs especially: two action bullets, never a descriptive paragraph.

## Review Checklist

Format-specific audit for one-pagers and leave-behinds. Run alongside the universal presentation checklist.

- [ ] **Fits one page.** Content renders on a single printed page (US Letter or A4) without overflow. Two pages acceptable for proposals.
- [ ] **Print renders clean.** Light background, dark text, no dark-canvas dependencies. `@media print` optimized for paper.
- [ ] **Grid layout.** Content organized in columns or grid, not single-column long-scroll flow.
- [ ] **Hierarchy unmistakable.** Eye path from headline → key stat → detail → CTA is obvious on first glance.
- [ ] **Structural labels present and human.** Each section has a short label (2-5 words, label voice) that identifies the section type. Labels are plain and conversational, not academic or cheeky. Reader can scan labels alone to understand the document flow.
- [ ] **No multi-sentence paragraphs.** Section intros are one sentence max; body content is bullets, cards, or stats, not prose runs. CTA is action bullets, not a description.
- [ ] **Ledes visually distinct.** Section intro lines render a clear tier above card/bullet body text (size or weight). Nothing reads as a flat text block.
- [ ] **No interactivity required.** No tabs, modals, or hover-dependent content. Everything visible statically.
- [ ] **Co-branding present.** Customer logo and Octave branding visible and professional.
- [ ] **CTA visible.** Next steps or call to action prominent, not buried.
- [ ] **No overflow.** No content clipped or hidden at page boundaries when printed.
