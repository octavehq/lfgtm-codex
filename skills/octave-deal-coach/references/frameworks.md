# Deal Coaching Framework — Resonate, Elevate, Compel

This reference defines three core deal coaching stages. Each stage produces three coaching outputs — **Buyer Mindset**, **Value Propositions**, and **Talking Points** — grounded in the seller's Octave library data.

When coaching, map every element to the seller's actual GTM messaging. Never deliver generic advice — always connect to specific deal context and library content.

---

## The Three Stages

| Stage | Label | Focus | Seller Role |
|-------|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Resonate** | Understand and resonate with the buyer | Diagnose the real problem, build trust, validate pain | Diagnostic expert |
| **Elevate** | Confirm the fit and elevate the opportunity | Disrupt status quo, differentiate, confirm solution fit | Strategic advisor |
| **Compel** | Deliver the value and compel the buyer to action | Quantify value, create urgency, arm the champion | Business case partner |

## Per-Stage Coaching Output

Every coaching output — whether a role play scorecard, microsite section, deck slide, or quiz answer — maps to three fields:

| Field | Type | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| **Buyer Mindset** | String | Where the buyer's head is — their psychology, fears, motivations, and decision frame at this stage |
| **Value Propositions** | Array | Which value props from the seller's library should be deployed, and why they fit this stage |
| **Talking Points** | Array | Specific things to say — grounded in deal context, playbook messaging, and proof points |

## Stage 1: Resonate

**Buyer's Journey Phase:** Explore
**Buyer Mindset:** Exploratory — the buyer is assessing whether they even have a problem worth solving. They're guarded, sharing surface symptoms. They need to feel understood before they'll go deeper.
**Coaching Agent:** Resonance Coach

### Overview

Resonate is about diagnosing the real problem before prescribing a solution. Most sellers jump straight to product demos. The Resonate stage says: you must first understand the buyer's current situation, desired outcomes, and the gap between them. The seller's job is to be a diagnostic expert — not a product pitcher.

The core tension at Resonate: the buyer shares surface problems; the seller must go deeper without pushing too hard.

### Key Evidence

- Buyers who feel understood before being pitched to are significantly more likely to advance deals
- A large share of deals stall because the seller addressed symptoms rather than root causes
- Problem framing determines solution framing — whoever defines the problem controls the sale

### Skills in Play

#### Discovery Principles

Good discovery follows three principles, applied fluidly throughout the conversation — not as a rigid checklist:

1. **Go wide** — Understand the full landscape before drilling into any one area. Who's involved? What's been tried? What triggered this conversation? Map the terrain.

2. **Go deep** — Find root causes, not symptoms. When the buyer says "our reporting takes too long," that's a symptom. The root cause might be fragmented data, manual processes, or organizational misalignment. Keep asking "why" and "what happens when."

3. **Go high** — Connect the problem to its impact across the organization. Understand how it affects the person doing the work daily, the leader accountable for the function, and the executive accountable to the board. The higher you go, the more urgency you create.

These principles can be applied in any order and revisited throughout the conversation. The skilled seller weaves them naturally — sometimes going wide, then deep on one thread, then high to connect it, then wide again to find another dimension.

### Buyer Mindset Assessment

At the Resonate stage, assess and document:

- **Awareness level:** Does the buyer know they have a problem, or are they just exploring?
- **Trigger:** What initiated this conversation — reactive (problem hit) or proactive (strategic initiative)?
- **Stakeholder dynamics:** Is this the real buyer, or were they told to "look into this"?
- **Prior attempts:** What have they tried? Why did it fail? This reveals hidden constraints.
- **Openness:** Are they sharing freely or guarded? What would make them open up?

### Value Propositions for Resonate

Select value props that:
- **Validate the pain** without jumping to solution — "we see this pattern in [industry]"
- **Expand the problem scope** — props that introduce dimensions the buyer hasn't considered
- **Build credibility** — props backed by industry benchmarks or proof points that show you understand their world
- **Avoid:** Solution-heavy props, ROI claims, competitive comparisons (those belong in Elevate and Compel)

### Talking Points for Resonate

**Opening — Lead with curiosity, not capability:**
> "Before I show you anything about [Product], I'd love to understand what's driving this conversation. What's happening in your world right now that made this a priority?"

**Going wide — Map the landscape:**
> "Who else is involved in this? What does this look like from their perspective?"

**Going deep — Find root causes:**
> "You mentioned [stated problem]. Help me understand — how long has this been going on? What have you tried so far to address it?"

**Going deep — Expand to unseen dimensions:**
> "That makes sense. What I'm hearing is [restate]. But I'm curious — when [problem] happens, what's the ripple effect across [related teams/processes]?"

**Going high — Connect to business impact:**
> "So if we zoom out — this isn't just about [tactical issue]. It sounds like this is really about [business impact: revenue, efficiency, risk]. Is that fair?"

**Going high — Connect to personal impact:**
> "When those decisions are delayed, how does that affect your standing with [leadership/board]?"

**Validating — Confirm the reframed problem:**
> "Let me play this back to make sure I have it right: [synthesized problem narrative covering multiple dimensions and stakeholders]. Does that capture it?"

**Transition to Elevate:**
> "Now that we're aligned on the problem, let me share some perspective on why this might be more urgent than it appears..."

### Signals to Listen For

| Signal | What It Means | Action |
|--------|---------------|--------|
| Vague problem statements | Need deeper diagnosis | Stay in Resonate — go deeper |
| "We've looked at a few things" | Prior failed attempts | Dig into what they tried and why it failed |
| "My boss asked me to look into this" | Delegated initiative — real buyer is elsewhere | Go wide — map the stakeholders; find the real decision maker |
| Specific metrics or deadlines | Urgency signal | Note for Compel stage; this buyer may be further along |
| Emotional language about the problem | Personal dimension engaged | This is gold — capture it for the Buyer Mindset |

## Stage 2: Elevate

**Buyer's Journey Phase:** Evaluate
**Buyer Mindset:** Status quo defender shifting to evaluator — the buyer's real competitor isn't another vendor, it's doing nothing. They need to see what they're missing and believe your differentiation.
**Coaching Agent:** Elevation Coach

### Overview

Elevate is the most critical stage. Research consistently shows that the single biggest competitor in any deal is "no decision" — the buyer defaulting to the status quo. Elevate's job is twofold:

1. **Disrupt the status quo** — Show the buyer that their current approach breaks under pressure
2. **Differentiate on value** — Position your unique capabilities in terms of what they MEAN, not what they ARE

The sequence matters: the case for change must come BEFORE differentiation. If you differentiate before disrupting, you're just another vendor pitching features.

### Key Evidence

- "No decision" is the most common deal outcome — more than any competitor
- Status quo bias is the strongest psychological barrier to buying
- Buyers who learn something new about their own situation are more likely to switch providers
- Sellers who lead with business outcomes over feature lists face significantly fewer price objections
- Buyers who understand what a capability means for their business are far more likely to champion internally

### Skills in Play

#### The Case for Change

Build the case for change through three narrative beats — not a rigid sequence, but a story arc that creates momentum:

1. **The Shift** — What's changing in the buyer's world that makes their current approach untenable. This combines market forces (regulatory changes, competitive pressure, technology shifts) with how those forces expose gaps in what they're doing today. The key: the current approach isn't bad — it was built for a world that no longer exists.

2. **The Stakes** — What happens if they don't adapt, and what they're not seeing. Quantify the cost of inaction using benchmarks and proof points. Then deliver the reframe — the insight the buyer didn't have before this conversation. "This isn't about [what they think]. It's about [what it's really about]." This is the moment that shifts their perspective.

3. **The Possibility** — What the new reality looks like concretely. Paint the future state using actual customer outcomes and specific metrics. Make it vivid, measurable, and emotionally compelling.

#### Value Framing

Most sellers pitch features. Strong sellers translate their product into the buyer's language. Think of it as a translation exercise across three audiences:

- **Product voice** — What the feature is. Keep this brief — nobody buys features.
- **Buyer voice** — How their day-to-day changes. This is where the buyer starts nodding. Speak in their workflow, their frustrations, their goals.
- **Executive voice** — What moves on the P&L. Revenue, cost, risk, speed. This is the language that gets budget approved.

Always lead with Buyer voice or Executive voice. Only drop into Product voice when asked "how does that work?"

#### Differentiated Value

Focus your differentiation on the intersection of three things:
1. What you do well
2. What the buyer actually needs
3. What competitors can't match

Only capabilities at that intersection are worth talking about. Everything else is either parity (everyone does it), irrelevant (you do it but they don't care), or a vulnerability (they need it but you can't deliver). Disciplined sellers stay focused on differentiated value and don't get pulled into feature-by-feature comparisons.

#### Proof Points

Specific evidence that makes claims believable. Instead of "we're the best," use:
- Named customers with specific metrics: "Acme Corp reduced [metric] by 34% in 90 days"
- Process details: "We deploy in 3 weeks, not 3 months, because [specific reason]"
- Scale markers: "47 of the Fortune 100 use this exact workflow"

### Buyer Mindset Assessment

At the Elevate stage, assess and document:

- **Status quo attachment:** How invested are they in their current approach? What would they lose by changing?
- **Change readiness:** Do they acknowledge the problem (from Resonate) is worth solving, or are they still hedging?
- **Evaluation mode:** Are they actively comparing alternatives, or still deciding whether to do anything?
- **Influence on criteria:** Can you still shape what they evaluate on, or are criteria locked?
- **Champion emergence:** Is someone internally starting to advocate for change?

### Value Propositions for Elevate

Select value props that:
- **Disrupt the status quo** — props that expose blind spots in the buyer's current approach
- **Show differentiated value** — props at the intersection of unique, important, and unmatched by competitors
- **Connect to outcomes** — props that show what the changed state looks like with specific proof
- **Avoid:** Generic feature comparisons, price-focused props, ROI calculations (save quantification for Compel)

### Talking Points for Elevate

**The Shift — Market forces:**
> "I've been talking to a lot of [industry] leaders recently, and there's a pattern I keep seeing. [External pressure]. How is that showing up for you?"

**The Shift — Status quo gaps:**
> "What I hear from most teams using [current approach] is that it was built for a world where [old assumption]. But now that [external pressure], that approach starts to break down because [specific flaw]."

**The Stakes — Cost of inaction:**
> "Here's what concerns me: companies that don't adapt to [pressure] within the next [timeframe] are seeing [quantified impact]. And the gap only widens."

**The Stakes — The reframe:**
> "So this actually isn't about [what they think]. It's really about [the blind spot — the insight they didn't have]. And that changes the equation entirely."

**The Possibility — Future state:**
> "Our customers who made this shift saw [specific outcome]. [Second outcome]. And — this is the one that surprises people — [unexpected benefit]."

**Value Framing delivery:**
> "One thing that's different about our approach is [Product voice — brief]. What that means in practice is [Buyer voice — how their day changes]. And the business outcome our customers see is [Executive voice — metric + timeframe]."

**Differentiated Value positioning:**
> "There are things every vendor in this space does well — [acknowledge parity]. Where we're fundamentally different is [differentiated capability]. And the reason that matters for your situation is [connected impact]."

**Perspective-shifting question:**
> "One question worth exploring with [competitor] is: [question that highlights a gap]. It's important because [why it matters to this buyer's specific situation]."

**Proof point:**
> "To put this in perspective: [specific customer] was in a similar situation. After the switch, they saw [metric improvement] within [timeframe]. I can connect you with their team."

### Signals to Listen For

| Signal | What It Means | Action |
|--------|---------------|--------|
| "We're happy with what we have" | Status quo not disrupted | Strengthen the Case for Change — focus on The Shift and The Stakes |
| "Your competitor does that too" | Differentiation not established | Shift to differentiated value; deploy proof points |
| "What makes you different?" | Buyer is in evaluation mode | Value Framing time — lead with Buyer/Executive voice |
| "We need to see a demo" | Ready to evaluate solution fit | Good signal — use the demo to reinforce value framing, not just show features |
| New stakeholder enters | Potential regression to Resonate | Re-diagnose with the new stakeholder before continuing |

## Stage 3: Compel

**Buyer's Journey Phases:** Justify, Decide, Close
**Buyer Mindset:** Analytical and risk-averse — the buyer may believe they need to change (Resonate) and that you're the right choice (Elevate), but they need to justify the spend and feel confident defending this decision.
**Coaching Agent:** Compel Coach

### Overview

Compel addresses two connected challenges:

1. **Quantify the value** — Build a business case that withstands CFO scrutiny
2. **Create urgency to act** — Make the case for WHY NOW, not just WHY

Many deals die between "yes, we should do this" and "yes, we're doing this now." Compel equips the seller to bridge that gap by building bulletproof justification and arming the champion.

### Key Evidence

- Deals with quantified business cases close significantly faster
- The majority of executive buyers say vendors fail to connect product capabilities to financial outcomes
- A large share of deals that reach the proposal stage still fail to close — most due to inability to justify internally
- "Why Now" is more important than "Why Us" at this stage — urgency drives commitment
- Executive buyers decide based on: strategic alignment, financial return, and risk mitigation

### Skills in Play

#### Business Case Building

Build the business case in two phases — always co-create with the buyer, never hand them a pre-built calculator:

**Phase 1: Value Discovery** — Find what matters to this buyer
- What KPIs does their team get measured on?
- If this project succeeds, what numbers change?
- What does their CFO care about most right now?
- What competing projects are vying for the same budget?

**Phase 2: Value Proof** — Build the math together
- **Before/After modeling:** Side-by-side comparison with quantified delta across time, cost, revenue, risk, and quality dimensions
- **Value Chain:** Map how value flows through their organization — capability → workflow improvement → business outcome → financial result. Keep the chain concrete and traceable.
- **ROI framing:** Investment vs. return, payback period, 3-year model. Use the buyer's own numbers wherever possible — ownership drives commitment.

#### The Why Now Case

Create urgency across two dimensions:

1. **Business urgency** — Why the company should act now, not next quarter:
   - Strategic alignment with current priorities and mandates
   - Cost of delay compounding over time (monthly burn rate)
   - Budget cycle timing and pricing windows
   - Competitive pressure requiring a response
   - Market windows that close if delayed

2. **Personal urgency** — Why the champion should push now:
   - Career visibility opportunity tied to a strategic initiative
   - New role mandate (first 90 days window)
   - Risk of being seen as behind the curve
   - Peer comparison ("the team at [reference customer] that solved this became the model for the org")

#### Champion Enablement

Arm the internal champion with everything they need to sell internally — adapt the format to what works for their organization:
- Executive summary the champion can present in their own words
- Financial impact model with the buyer's numbers
- Risk framing — make NOT buying feel riskier than buying (asymmetric framing)
- Talk track for the executive conversation
- Objection responses for every likely internal pushback
- Offer to facilitate exec-to-exec alignment when appropriate

#### De-Risking Tactics

Reduce perceived risk at the decision point:
- Layer multiple proof points for credibility (analyst report + customer reference + pilot data)
- Offer a phase-gate approach — break the commitment into stages with clear milestones
- Provide implementation commitments with specific timelines
- Propose a focused pilot on a high-value use case before full commitment
- Frame the decision asymmetrically: the risk of NOT acting should feel greater than the risk of acting

### Buyer Mindset Assessment

At the Compel stage, assess and document:

- **Decision process:** Who signs off? What's the approval chain? Is budget available or needs creation?
- **Champion strength:** Can the champion sell internally, or do they need heavy coaching?
- **Competing priorities:** What other projects are fighting for the same budget?
- **Risk appetite:** Is the buyer more afraid of making a bad choice, or missing an opportunity?
- **Timeline pressure:** Is there a compelling event (deadline, fiscal year, board meeting)?
- **Detractors:** Who opposes this internally? What's their objection?

### Value Propositions for Compel

Select value props that:
- **Quantify impact** — props with specific ROI metrics from similar customers
- **Align to strategy** — props that map to the buyer's stated strategic priorities
- **Reduce risk** — props backed by implementation guarantees, phase-gate approaches, or pilot data
- **Enable the champion** — props the champion can present in their own words to executives
- **Avoid:** Discovery-stage props, generic positioning (those belong in Resonate and Elevate)

### Talking Points for Compel

**Value Discovery — Uncover metrics:**
> "If this project delivers everything you're hoping for, what numbers actually change on your dashboard? I want to make sure we're building the case around metrics that matter to your leadership."

**Value Proof — Paint the future:**
> "Let me paint a picture of what this looks like in practice. Today, your team spends [current effort] on [process]. That means [consequence]. In the new model, [future state]. That frees up [resource] for [higher-value activity]."

**Value Proof — Quantify:**
> "Let's put numbers on this. If [metric] improves by [percentage] — which is conservative based on what [similar customer] achieved — that translates to [dollar impact] over [timeframe]. Against an investment of [cost], that's a [X]:1 return in year one."

**Value Chain:**
> "Here's how the value chains: [capability] reduces [input] by [amount], which means [team] can [do what] [amount faster], which drives [business outcome] worth [dollar amount] annually."

**Business urgency:**
> "From what you've shared, [company] has [strategic priority] as a top initiative this year. This project directly accelerates that. If you wait until next quarter, you're [specific consequence of delay]."

**Business urgency — Cost of delay:**
> "Every month this isn't in place, you're spending [monthly cost]. Over 12 months, that's [annual cost]. The investment to solve it is [cost] with a [X]-month payback. The math gets worse the longer you wait."

**Personal urgency:**
> "As [champion's role], delivering this initiative gives you visibility on [strategic priority]. The team that solved this at [reference customer] became the model for the rest of the org."

**Arming the champion:**
> "When you present this to [executive], here's what I'd recommend: Lead with [strategic alignment]. Then the numbers: [financial summary]. Close with: [risk of not acting]. If they push back on timing, the answer is [Why Now response]."

**De-risking:**
> "To make this as low-risk as possible: [Phase-gate proposal — e.g., 'Start with a focused pilot on [use case], measure against [KPIs] at 60 days, then expand']. You get proof before full commitment."

### Signals to Listen For

| Signal | What It Means | Action |
|--------|---------------|--------|
| "It's too expensive" | Value not established (regression to Elevate) | Rebuild the value case before discussing price |
| "We need more time" | Urgency not created | Strengthen the Why Now Case — both dimensions |
| "My boss won't approve" | Champion not armed | Build the Executive Decision Kit |
| "Can you do anything on price?" | Value gap — NOT a negotiation signal yet | Trace back: is the business case quantified? |
| "What's the ROI?" | Buyer ready for business case | Great signal — co-create the numbers |
| "We're going with [competitor]" | Differentiation failed (regression to Elevate) | Return to differentiated value + proof points |

## Cross-Stage Principles

### The Golden Thread

Every deal maintains a narrative thread connecting:
- The **problem** (Resonate) → the **urgency and differentiation** (Elevate) → the **justification and action** (Compel)

If the thread breaks, the deal stalls. The coaching agent's job is to identify where it broke and help repair it.

### Stage Regression

Deals don't always move forward. Watch for regression signals:

| Regression | Trigger | Resolution |
|------------|---------|------------|
| Elevate → Resonate | New stakeholder enters who doesn't feel the problem | Re-diagnose with the new stakeholder |
| Compel → Elevate | Competitor re-engages with new positioning, or buyer questions differentiation | Rebuild differentiated value |
| Compel → Resonate | Organizational change (re-org, new exec, acquisition) | Start fresh with new stakeholder mapping |

When regression happens, return to the appropriate stage and rebuild. Don't try to skip ahead.

### Buyer Psychology Progression

| Stage | Buyer State | Primary Emotion | What Moves Them |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Resonate | Curious but guarded | Trust | Feeling understood, seeing that you "get it" |
| Elevate | Comfortable → disrupted | Urgency + confidence | Seeing blind spots, believing in differentiation |
| Compel | Analytical, risk-averse | Courage to commit | Quantified value, personal career alignment, reduced risk |
