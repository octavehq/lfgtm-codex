# Meeting-Prep Output Rules

The universal output rules live in the shared layer and are mandatory for this skill:

- [`editorial-rules.md`](../../shared/editorial-rules.md) — language quality, no tool terminology, speculation marking
- [`information-principles.md`](../../shared/information-principles.md) — structure, no redundancy, confirmed-vs-hypothesized tagging, outcome-not-process framing, pain-vs-deal-risk separation
- [`presentation-principles.md`](../../shared/presentation-principles.md) — no text walls, scannability, visual rules

This file adds only the rules specific to meeting prep.

## The Rules

### 1. Every data point gets a label
No orphan values. "$500,000" is meaningless; "Deal Value: $500,000" is information. Every number, date, name, or metric must be accompanied by a label that tells the reader what it is.

### 2. Every section gets a one-line intro
Before presenting content, explain what this section is and why it's here. One sentence at the top of each section that tells the reader what they're looking at and how to use it.

### 3. The header answers three questions
1. What is this document?
2. Who is it about?
3. What's the context?

Not branding, not tool names, not raw metadata. The header is a frame that orients the reader in under 5 seconds.

### 4. Discovery questions must be L2 or L3 and Octave-grounded
L1 diagnostic questions ("Walk me through how you handle X today") are table stakes anyone could ask after 5 minutes on the company's website. They don't belong in a prep document. Every discovery question must be:
- **L2 (forcing-function):** forces a binary choice that advances the conversation regardless of answer. "Does X trigger Y, or does it stay invisible?"
- **L3 (consequence):** surfaces the business cost of the status quo. "When your agent pitches against CBRE for a listing and the seller asks how you'll track buyer engagement, what does that answer look like today?"
- **Octave-grounded:** built from specific intelligence (deal pipeline data, enrichment, competitive signals, prior findings) that the seller couldn't have without Octave. If the question could be written by someone who just Googled the company, it doesn't belong.

3-4 questions maximum across the entire document. Fewer and sharper beats many and generic.

### 5. Persona cards are briefings, not dossiers
A persona card tells the seller who this person is, what pressure they're under, and why our product matters to them. That's it. No sub-sections for "How we make them the hero," "Top use cases," "Don't lead with" stacked inside persona cards. Keep each card to 2-3 rows: Pressure + Why [Product]. Use cases and messaging guidance belong in the messaging or strategy sections.
